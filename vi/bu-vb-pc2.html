Bhikkhu Vibhanga - Pācittiya 2: The Training Rule on Abusive Speech.

  • Translated from the Pali by Brahmali Bhikkhu.

Origin story

1At one time the Buddha, the Master, was staying at Sāvatthī in the Jeta Grove, Anāthapiṇḍika’s Monastery. At that time some monks belonging to the group of six were arguing with and abusing good monks. They reviled and insulted them about their caste, name, family, work, trade, illnesses, physical traits, defilements, and offences, and by calling them names. The monks of few desires … complained and criticised them: “How can those monks belonging to the group of six argue with and abuse good monks? How can they revile and insult them about their caste, name, family, work, trade, illnesses, physical traits, defilements, and offences, and by calling them names?"

2 After criticising those monks in many ways, they informed the Master. … “Is it true, monks, that you treat good monks in this way?" “It’s true, Master." The Buddha, the Master, criticised them: “ … Foolish men, how can you argue with and abuse good monks? How can you revile and insult them about their caste, name, family, work, trade, illnesses, physical traits, defilements, and offences, and by calling them names? This will not give rise to confidence in those without it … " … and after criticising them … he spoke on the Dhamma and addressed the monks:

3 “Once upon a time, monks, there was a brahman in Takkasilā who had an ox called Nandivisāla. On one occasion the ox said to the brahman: “Go, brahman, and make the following bet worth one thousand coins with the head merchant: ‘My ox will pull one hundred carts tied together.’" And that brahman did just that. Then, after tying one hundred carts together and yoking Nandivisāla to them, he said: “Go, you fraud; pull, you liar." But Nandivisāla just stood right there.

Then that brahman became depressed because he had lost one thousand coins. And Nandivisāla said to him: “Why are you depressed?"

“Because I lost one thousand coins because of you."

“But why did you disgrace me by calling me a fraud when I’m not? Now go, brahman, and make the same bet worth two thousand coins with the head merchant. But don’t disgrace me by calling me a fraud." Once again that brahman did just that. Then, after tying one hundred carts together and yoking Nandivisāla to them, he said: “Go, good ox; pull, good ox." And Nandivisāla pulled those one hundred carts.

4 “One should say what is pleasant,
Never what is unpleasant.
For him of pleasant speech,
Heavy loads got carried,
And he obtained wealth;
And he was delighted with that.

“Even at that time, monks, reviling and insulting was unpleasant to me. How then could reviling and insulting be pleasant now? This will not give rise to confidence in those without it … And, monks, this training rule should be recited thus:

Final ruling

6 “If a monk speaks abusively, he commits an offence entailing confession."

Definitions

7Speaks abusively: he abuses in ten ways: about caste, about name, about family, about work, about trade, about illnesses, about physical traits, about defilements, about offences, and by insulting.

Permutations

Definitions

8 Caste: there are two kinds of castes: low castes and high castes. Low castes: outcasts, bamboo workers, hunters, chariot-makers, waste-removers. These are called low castes. High castes: nobles and brahmans. These are called high castes.

9 Name: there are two kinds of names: low names and high names. Low names: Avakaṇṇaka, Javakaṇṇaka, Dhaniṭṭhaka, Saviṭṭhaka, Kulavaḍḍhaka, or names, in whatever countries, that are despised, looked down upon, scorned, treated with contempt, disregarded. These are called low names. High names: connected with the Buddha, connected with the Dhamma, connected with the Sangha, or names, in whatever countries, that are highly regarded, highly thought of, respected, valued, esteemed. These are called high names.

10Family: there are two kinds of families: low families and high families. Low families: the Kosiya family, the Bhāradvāja family, or families, in whatever countries, that are despised, looked down upon, scorned, treated with contempt, disregarded. These are called low families. High families: the Gotama family, the Moggallāna family, the Kaccāna family, the Vāsiṭṭha family, or families, in whatever countries, that are highly regarded, highly thought of, respected, valued, esteemed. These are called high families.

11Work: there are two kinds of work: low work and high work. Low work: carpentry, waste-removing, or work, in whatever countries, that is despised, looked down upon, scorned, treated with contempt, disregarded. This is called low work. High work: farming, trade, cattle-keeping, or work, in whatever countries, that is highly regarded, highly thought of, respected, valued, esteemed. This is called high work.

12Occupation: there are two kinds of occupations: low occupations and high occupations. Low occupations: reed-work, pottery, weaving, leather-work, hairdressing, or occupations, in whatever countries, that are despised, looked down upon, scorned, treated with contempt, disregarded. These are called low occupations. High occupations: calculating, accounting, writing, or occupations, in whatever countries, that are highly regarded, highly thought of, respected, valued, esteemed. These are called high occupations.

13Illnesses: all are low, but there is the disease of diabetes which is high.

14Physical traits: there are two kinds of physical traits: low physical traits and high physical traits. Low physical traits: too tall, too short, too dark, too fair. These are called low physical traits. High physical traits: not too tall, not too short, not too dark, not too fair. These are called high physical traits.

15Defilements: all are low.

16Offences: all are low, but there is the attainment of stream-entry which is high. The English translation cannot properly capture the Pali, in which the word for ‘offence’ and ‘attainment’ is the same, āpatti.

17Insults: there are two kinds of insults: low insults and high insults. Low insults: you are a camel, you are a ram, you are an ox, you are a donkey, you are an animal, you are bound for hell; you are not going to a good destination, you can only expect a bad destination; adding the disparaging endings ya or bha to someone’s name, or calling someone words for the male and female genitals. These are called low insults. High insults: you are wise, you are competent, you are intelligent, you are learned, you are a Dhamma-speaker; you are not going to a bad destination, you can only expect a good destination. These are called high insults.

71If one who is fully ordained, not wishing to revile, not wishing to insult, not wishing to humiliate another who is fully ordained, but because he wants to have fun, says what is high to one who is low—an outcast, a bamboo worker, a hunter, a chariot-maker, a waste-remover—saying, “You are a noble, you are a brahman," then for every statement, he commits an offence of bad speech.

72If one who is fully ordained, not wishing to revile, not wishing to insult, not wishing to humiliate another who is fully ordained, but because he wants to have fun, says what is high to one who is high—a noble, a brahman—saying, “You are a noble, you are a brahman," then for every statement, he commits an offence of bad speech.

73If one who is fully ordained, not wishing to revile, not wishing to insult, not wishing to humiliate another who is fully ordained, but because he wants to have fun, says what is low to one who is low … says what is low to one who is high … says what is high to one who is low … says what is high to one who is high—one who is wise, one who is competent, one who is intelligent, one who is learned, one who is a Dhamma-speaker—saying, “You are wise, you are competent, you are intelligent, you are learned, you are a Dhamma-speaker; you are not going to a bad destination, you can only expect a good destination," then for every statement, he commits an offence of bad speech.

Not intending to abuse, indirect speech

74If one who is fully ordained, not wishing to revile, not wishing to insult, not wishing to humiliate another who is fully ordained, but because he wants to have fun, says this: “There are outcasts, bamboo workers, hunters, chariot-makers, waste-removers, right here" … “There are wise ones, competent ones, intelligent ones, learned ones, Dhamma-speakers, right here; they are not going to a bad destination, they can only expect a good destination," then for every statement, he commits an offence of bad speech.

75If one who is fully ordained, not wishing to revile, not wishing to insult, not wishing to humiliate another who is fully ordained, but because he wants to have fun, says this: “Perhaps these are outcasts, bamboo workers, hunters, chariot-makers, waste-removers" … “Perhaps these are wise ones, competent ones, intelligent ones, learned ones, Dhamma-speakers," then for every statement, he commits an offence of bad speech.

76If one who is fully ordained, not wishing to revile, not wishing to insult, not wishing to humiliate another who is fully ordained, but because he wants to have fun, says this: “We are not outcasts, bamboo workers, hunters, chariot-makers, waste-removers" … “We are not wise ones, competent ones, intelligent ones, learned ones, Dhamma-speakers; we are not going to a bad destination, we can only expect a good destination," then for every statement, he commits an offence of bad speech.

Not intending to abuse one who is not fully ordained

77If one who is fully ordained, not wishing to revile, not wishing to insult, not wishing to humiliate someone who is not fully ordained, but because he wants to have fun, says what is low to one who is low … says what is low to one who is high … says what is high to one who is low … says what is high to one who is high—one who is wise, one who is competent, one who is intelligent, one who is learned, one who is a Dhamma-speaker—saying, “You are wise, you are competent, you are intelligent, you are learned, you are a Dhamma-speaker; you are not going to a bad destination, you can only expect a good destination," then for every statement, he commits an offence of bad speech.

80If one who is fully ordained, not wishing to revile, not wishing to insult, not wishing to humiliate someone who is not fully ordained, but because he wants to have fun, says this: “We are not outcasts, bamboo workers, hunters, chariot-makers, waste-removers" … “We are not wise ones, competent ones, intelligent ones, learned ones, Dhamma-speakers; we are not going to a bad destination, we can only expect a good destination," then for every statement, he commits an offence of bad speech

Non-offences

81There is no offence: if he is aiming at what is beneficial; if he is aiming at teaching the Dhamma; if he is aiming at giving instruction; if he is insane; if he is deranged; if he is overwhelmed by pain; if he is the first offender.
